DDPM math and code

This post aims to provide an overview of the math behind Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) and how to implement them in code. The focus will be on the key equations and their implementation in PyTorch.

Mathematical Formulation

Forward Process

The forward diffusion can be written in both probabilistic and deterministic forms. The probabilistic form is given by:

q(xt∣xt−1)=N(xt;αtxt−1,βtI)q(xt∣x0)=N(xt;αˉtx0,βˉtI)\begin{aligned} q(x_t | x_{t-1}) &= \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \\ q(x_t | x_0) &= \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, \bar{\beta}_t I) \end{aligned} q(xt​∣xt−1​)q(xt​∣x0​)​=N(xt​;αt​​xt−1​,βt​I)=N(xt​;αˉt​​x0​,βˉ​t​I)​

here βt\beta_tβt​ is the noise schedule, αˉt=∏s=1t(1−βs)\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - \beta_s)αˉt​=∏s=1t​(1−βs​), and αt=1−βt\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_tαt​=1−βt​, βˉt=1−αˉt\bar{\beta}_t = 1 - \bar{\alpha}_tβˉ​t​=1−αˉt​.

The deterministic form starts from:

xt=αtxt+1−αtϵ , ϵ∼N(0,I)=αt(αt−1xt−1+1−αt−1ϵ)+1−αtϵ=... (reorganize the variances of the standard noise ϵ)=αˉtx0+1−αˉtϵ\begin{aligned} x_t &= \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_t + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t} \epsilon \text{ , } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\\ &= \sqrt{\alpha_t} (\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}} x_{t-1} + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_{t-1}} \epsilon) + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t} \epsilon \\ &= ... \text{ (reorganize the variances of the standard noise $\epsilon$)}\\ &= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \epsilon \end{aligned} xt​​=αt​​xt​+1−αt​​ϵ , ϵ∼N(0,I)=αt​​(αt−1​​xt−1​+1−αt−1​​ϵ)+1−αt​​ϵ=... (reorganize the variances of the standard noise ϵ)=αˉt​​x0​+1−αˉt​​ϵ​

Reverse Process

The reverse process aims to train a model that can denoise the noisy samples xtx_txt​ back to the original data x0x_0x0​. Which means that we want to minimize the KL divergence between the true posterior q(xt−1∣xt)q(x_{t-1} | x_t)q(xt−1​∣xt​) and the model’s approximation p(xt−1∣xt)p(x_{t-1} | x_t)p(xt−1​∣xt​).
The true posterior is given by:

q(xt−1∣xt)=q(xt∣xt−1)q(xt−1∣x0)q(xt∣x0)=N(xt;αtxt−1,βtI)N(xt−1;αˉt−1x0,βˉt−1I)N(xt;αˉtx0,βˉtI)\begin{aligned} q(x_{t-1} | x_t) &= \frac{q(x_t | x_{t-1}) q(x_{t-1} | x_0)}{q(x_t | x_0)} \\ &= \frac{\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} x_0, \bar{\beta}_{t-1} I)}{\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, \bar{\beta}_t I)} \end{aligned} q(xt−1​∣xt​)​=q(xt​∣x0​)q(xt​∣xt−1​)q(xt−1​∣x0​)​=N(xt​;αˉt​​x0​,βˉ​t​I)N(xt​;αt​​xt−1​,βt​I)N(xt−1​;αˉt−1​​x0​,βˉ​t−1​I)​​

This is because we don’t know q(xt)q(x_t)q(xt​) and q(xt−1)q(x_{t-1})q(xt−1​) directly, but we can compute q(xt−1∣x0)q(x_{t-1} | x_0)q(xt−1​∣x0​) and q(xt∣x0)q(x_t | x_0)q(xt​∣x0​) in closed form.

Implementation in PyTorch

Warning: The following code is a simplified version of the OpenAI’s official implementation, which is more complex and includes many optimizations. The code here is meant to illustrate the key concepts and equations and for better readability.

Beta Schedule:

The beta schedule is crucial for the forward diffusion process. It defines how the noise is added to the data at each step. Common schedules include linear and cosine. People prefer cosine schedules because they provide a smoother transition at the endpoints (the beginning and end of the diffusion process), when the noise is very small or very large. This helps to stabilize the training process and improve the quality of the generated samples.

Positional Embeddings in Transformers

Posted on 2025-05-07 Edited on 2025-05-09 In Generative models

Positional Embeddings in Vasvani et al. (2017)

The original Transformer model proposed by Vasvani et al. (2017) uses sinusoidal positional embeddings. The idea is to encode the position of each token in the sequence using a combination of sine and cosine functions with different frequencies. The formula for the positional embedding is as follows:

PE(pos,2i)=sin⁡(pos100002idmodel)PE(pos,2i+1)=cos⁡(pos100002idmodel)\begin{aligned} PE_{(pos, 2i)} &= \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{\frac{2i}{d_{model}}}}\right) \\ PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} &= \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{\frac{2i}{d_{model}}}}\right) \end{aligned} PE(pos,2i)​PE(pos,2i+1)​​=sin(10000dmodel​2i​pos​)=cos(10000dmodel​2i​pos​)​

where pospospos is the position of the token in the sequence, iii is the dimension index, and dmodeld_{model}dmodel​ is the dimensionality of the model. The sine and cosine functions are used to ensure that each position has a unique representation, and the different frequencies allow the model to learn relationships between tokens at different distances.

Relative Positional Embeddings: Shaw et al. (2018) / Huang et al. (2018)

The key idea of relative positional embeddings is to encode the relative distances between tokens rather than their absolute positions. They are computed on the fly during the attention computation:

ei,j=xiWQ(xjWK+ai,jK)Tdke_{i,j} = \frac{x_i W^Q(x_j W^K + a^K_{i,j})^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} ei,j​=dk​​xi​WQ(xj​WK+ai,jK​)T​

The attention weights are then:

αi,j=exp⁡(ei,j)∑k=1nexp⁡(ei,k)\alpha_{i,j} = \frac{\exp(e_{i,j})}{\sum_{k=1}^{n} \exp(e_{i,k})}αi,j​=∑k=1n​exp(ei,k​)exp(ei,j​)​

The output of the attention layer is computed as:

zi=∑j=1nαi,j(xjWV+ai,jV)z_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \alpha_{i,j} (x_j W^V + a^V_{i,j})zi​=j=1∑n​αi,j​(xj​WV+ai,jV​)

where ai,jKa^K_{i,j}ai,jK​ and ai,jVa^V_{i,j}ai,jV​ are the relative positional embeddings for the key and value respectively. The relative positional embeddings are learned during training and allow the model to focus on tokens that are close to each other in the sequence.

However, Shaw et al. (2018) implementation requires O(L2D)O(L^2D)O(L2D) memory. In Huang et al. (2018), the authors reformulated relative attention to the following:

RelativeAttention(Q,K,V)=Softmax(QKT+QRTdk)VRelativeAttention(Q, K, V) = Softmax\left(\frac{QK^T + QR^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V RelativeAttention(Q,K,V)=Softmax(dk​​QKT+QRT​)V

where Ri,j=ai,jKR_{i,j} = a^K_{i,j}Ri,j​=ai,jK​, which means the above formulation is equivalent to the Shaw et al. (2018) implementation. Huang et al. avoided the computation of RRR by using a ErE_rEr​ matrix, which is a learned embedding of the relative distance between tokens.

AliBi: Attention with Linear Biases (Beltagy et al. 2020)

The key idea is to use a linear bias term that depends on the distance between tokens, rather than using learned embeddings. The bias term is similar in the relative positional embeddings, but it is computed using a linear function of the distance between tokens.

This is very easy to implement and can extended to longer sequences.

RoPE: Rotary Positional Embedding (Su et al. 2021)

The key idea is to rotate the query and key representations with an angle proportional to their absolute positions before applying the dot product attention.

Relative Position Bias in Swin Transformer (Liu et al. 2021)

The relative position bias is added to the attention scores before applying the softmax function. The attention score is similar to previous formulations:

Attention(Q,K,V)=Softmax(QKT+Bdk)VAttention(Q, K, V) = Softmax\left(\frac{QK^T + B}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V Attention(Q,K,V)=Softmax(dk​​QKT+B​)V

Fourier Position Embedding (Hua et al. 2025)

Key theory is that RoPE implicitly performs Non-Uniform Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) on the input sequence. First, given a sequence x1,x2,…,xn{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n}x1​,x2​,…,xn​, DFT converts the sequence into the frequency domain with equally spaced frequencies. The DFT is defined as:

Xk=∑n=0N−1xne−2πiknNX_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n e^{-2\pi i \frac{kn}{N}}Xk​=n=0∑N−1​xn​e−2πiNkn​

where XkX_kXk​ is the DFT of the sequence, xnx_nxn​ is the input sequence, and kkk is the frequency index. The frequency sequence is then ωk=2πkN\omega_k = \frac{2\pi k}{N}ωk​=N2πk​. Non-Uniform DFT (NUDFT) is a generalization of DFT that allows for non-uniformly spaced frequencies with ωk∼U[0,2π)\omega_k \sim \mathcal{U}[0, 2\pi)ωk​∼U[0,2π).

An intersting phenomenon in the paper is the spectrum damage, which is demonstrated both inside and outside attention. The inside attention case is discussed in Peng et al. 2023, where the authors show that when the lower frequency components are not fully covered, positions exceeding the coverage performs bad in generalization. The spectrum damage outside attention includes the spectrum leakage in linear layers, and the spectrum distortion in the attention mechanism. The authors show that the spectrum damage can be mitigated by using a linear layer with a non-uniformly spaced frequency.

Posted on 2025-02-12 Edited on 2025-05-04 In DL Heuristics

Simple moving average (SMA)

In financial math, the SMA is simply the moving average of previous k data points.

SMAk=pn−k+1+pn−k+2+...+pnk=1k∑i=0k−1pn−i\text{SMA}_k = \frac{p_{n-k+1}+p_{n-k+2}+...+p_{n}}{k} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} p_{n-i} SMAk​=kpn−k+1​+pn−k+2​+...+pn​​=k1​i=0∑k−1​pn−i​

where pnp_npn​ is the price at time n.

Exponential moving average (EMA)

The EMA is a weighted average of previous data points, where the weights decrease exponentially. The formula for EMA is given by:

EMAn=αpn+(1−α)EMAn−1\text{EMA}_n = \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-1}EMAn​=αpn​+(1−α)EMAn−1​

where α\alphaα is the smoothing factor, which determines the weight given to the most recent data point. The value of α\alphaα is typically between 0 and 1, and it can be calculated as:

α=2N+1\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1} α=N+12​

where N is the number of periods over which the EMA is calculated.

Why is it exponential? Reformulating the equation we can see that:

EMAn=αpn+(1−α)EMAn−1=αpn+(1−α)(αpn−1+(1−α)EMAn−2)=αpn+(1−α)αpn−1+(1−α)2EMAn−2=αpn+(1−α)αpn−1+(1−α)2αpn−2+...=∑i=0∞(1−α)iαpn−i\begin{aligned} \text{EMA}_n &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-1} \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \left( \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-2} \right) \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha)^2 \text{EMA}_{n-2} \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha)^2 \alpha p_{n-2} + ... \\ &= \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} (1-\alpha)^i \alpha p_{n-i} \\ \end{aligned} EMAn​​=αpn​+(1−α)EMAn−1​=αpn​+(1−α)(αpn−1​+(1−α)EMAn−2​)=αpn​+(1−α)αpn−1​+(1−α)2EMAn−2​=αpn​+(1−α)αpn−1​+(1−α)2αpn−2​+...=i=0∑∞​(1−α)iαpn−i​​

Here the shared weight α\alphaα is multiplied by the exponentially decreasing factor (1−α)i(1-\alpha)^i(1−α)i. Meaning that the previous data has a weight that decreases exponentially with time.

Why is it useful in deep learning?

Weight Update with EMA

θEMA=αθ+(1−α)θEMA\theta_{EMA} = \alpha \theta + (1-\alpha) \theta_{EMA} θEMA​=αθ+(1−α)θEMA​

where θ\thetaθ is the model’s parameters, and θEMA\theta_{EMA}θEMA​ is the EMA of the model’s parameters.

First of all, the optimization of a neural network is a non-convex problem. The loss function is not smooth and has many local minima or saddle points. Thus, SGD adds noise to the optimization process, which can help escape local minima and saddle points. However, this noise can also be harmful to the model’s performance. The EMA can help smooth out the noise and provide a more stable estimate of the model’s parameters.

EMA is also particularly useful when the training process is highly stochastic by the nature of the data, the model architecture, or the training process itself. For example, in self-supervised learning, the model is trained on a large amount of unlabeled data, which can be highly variable and noisy. The EMA can help stabilize the training process and improve the model’s performance.

  • Deep Learning Book
  • How to scale your EMA. Busbridge et al. NeurIPS 2023

In Reinforcement Learning

In RL, for methods such as DDPG, the trajectories are sampled from a replay buffer collected from the main model. This coupling between the main model and the replay buffer can lead to high variance in the updates of the value function and policy. To reduce this variance, the EMA is used to maintain a separate target model that is updated with a moving average of the main model’s parameters. This target model is used to compute the target Q-values for the main model, which helps to stabilize the training process and reduce the variance in the updates.

  • OpenAI - DDPG

In GANs

In GANs, the generator and discriminator are trained in an adversarial manner. The generator tries to generate realistic samples while the discriminator tries to distinguish between real and fake samples. This can lead to instability in the training process. To stabilize the training, EMA is used for the generator’s parameters to smooth out the noise.

  • StyleGAN paper
  • BigGAN paper

In Vision Transformers (DINO) and Diffusion Transformers

In ViTs and DiTs, the training process is highly unstable due to the large number of parameters and the self-supervised training.

In DINO, the teacher weights are updated with EMA of the student weights. The author mentioned that this EMA formulates a model-ensembling similar to the Polyak-Ruppert averaging with an exponential decay, which was a standard technique in the past to improve model performance.

In DiT, the EMA is assumed to eliminate the overfitting of the model to the last seen batch, thus the learned score function is more generalized to the whole dataset. This usually improves the similarity between the generated distribution and the true distribution.

  • DINO paper
  • DiT paper

Classifier Free Guidance for Diffusion models

Posted on 2024-11-20 Edited on 2025-06-06 In Generative models

Classifier Guidance

Diffusion models predict score functions ∇xlog⁡p(x)\nabla_x \log{p(x)}∇x​logp(x). If the input contains conditional information such as class label, text, or image embeddings, the new score function is ∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)\nabla_x \log{p(x|y)}∇x​logp(x∣y). By Bayes rule, the new score function can be written as

∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)=∇xlog⁡p(y∣x)+∇xlog⁡p(x).\nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} = \nabla_x \log{p(y|x)} + \nabla_x \log{p(x)}. ∇x​logp(x∣y)=∇x​logp(y∣x)+∇x​logp(x).

From here we notice that ∇xlog⁡p(y∣x)\nabla_x \log{p(y|x)}∇x​logp(y∣x) is exactly the log gradient of a discriminative model, or we can call it a classifier. Given a unconditional diffusion model that predicts ∇xlog⁡p(x)\nabla_x \log{p(x)}∇x​logp(x), and a classifier that allows us to compute ∇xlog⁡p(y∣x)\nabla_x \log{p(y|x)}∇x​logp(y∣x), we have the formulation for Classifier Guidance:

∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)=∇xlog⁡p(x)+γ∇xlog⁡p(y∣x),\nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} = \nabla_x \log{p(x)} + \gamma\nabla_x \log{p(y|x)}, ∇x​logp(x∣y)=∇x​logp(x)+γ∇x​logp(y∣x),

where γ\gammaγ is the guidance scale.

However, it is expensive to train another classifier. Can we guide our diffusion model without additional training?

Classifier Free Guidance

Noticing that we have

∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)=∇xlog⁡p(y∣x)+∇xlog⁡p(x),∇xlog⁡p(y∣x)=∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)−∇xlog⁡p(x).\begin{aligned} \nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} = & \nabla_x \log{p(y|x)} + \nabla_x \log{p(x)}, \\ \nabla_x \log{p(y|x)} = & \nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} - \nabla_x \log{p(x)}. \end{aligned}∇x​logp(x∣y)=∇x​logp(y∣x)=​∇x​logp(y∣x)+∇x​logp(x),∇x​logp(x∣y)−∇x​logp(x).​

Plug this back into the formula of Classifier Guidance:

∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)=∇xlog⁡p(x)+γ∇xlog⁡p(y∣x),=∇xlog⁡p(x)+γ(∇xlog⁡p(x∣y)−∇xlog⁡p(x)),=(1−γ)∇xlog⁡p(x)+γ∇xlog⁡p(x∣y).\begin{aligned} \nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} = &\nabla_x \log{p(x)} + \gamma\nabla_x \log{p(y|x)}, \\ = & \nabla_x \log{p(x)} + \gamma(\nabla_x \log{p(x|y)} - \nabla_x \log{p(x)}), \\ = & (1-\gamma)\nabla_x \log{p(x)} + \gamma\nabla_x \log{p(x|y)}. \end{aligned}∇x​logp(x∣y)===​∇x​logp(x)+γ∇x​logp(y∣x),∇x​logp(x)+γ(∇x​logp(x∣y)−∇x​logp(x)),(1−γ)∇x​logp(x)+γ∇x​logp(x∣y).​

Normally, we train a conditioned diffusion model with an additional Null class embedding, which corresponds to the unconditional diffusion case. Thus, we only need to train the model once with true class embeddings randomly replaced by this Null class embedding.
