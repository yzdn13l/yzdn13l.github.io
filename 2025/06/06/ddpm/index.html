DDPM math and code

This post aims to provide an overview of the math behind Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) and how to implement them in code. The focus will be on the key equations and their implementation in PyTorch.

Mathematical Formulation

Forward Process

The forward diffusion can be written in both probabilistic and deterministic forms. The probabilistic form is given by:

q(xt∣xt−1)=N(xt;αtxt−1,βtI)q(xt∣x0)=N(xt;αˉtx0,βˉtI)\begin{aligned} q(x_t | x_{t-1}) &= \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \\ q(x_t | x_0) &= \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, \bar{\beta}_t I) \end{aligned} q(xt​∣xt−1​)q(xt​∣x0​)​=N(xt​;αt​​xt−1​,βt​I)=N(xt​;αˉt​​x0​,βˉ​t​I)​

here βt\beta_tβt​ is the noise schedule, αˉt=∏s=1t(1−βs)\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1 - \beta_s)αˉt​=∏s=1t​(1−βs​), and αt=1−βt\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_tαt​=1−βt​, βˉt=1−αˉt\bar{\beta}_t = 1 - \bar{\alpha}_tβˉ​t​=1−αˉt​.

The deterministic form starts from:

xt=αtxt+1−αtϵ , ϵ∼N(0,I)=αt(αt−1xt−1+1−αt−1ϵ)+1−αtϵ=... (reorganize the variances of the standard noise ϵ)=αˉtx0+1−αˉtϵ\begin{aligned} x_t &= \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_t + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t} \epsilon \text{ , } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\\ &= \sqrt{\alpha_t} (\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}} x_{t-1} + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_{t-1}} \epsilon) + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t} \epsilon \\ &= ... \text{ (reorganize the variances of the standard noise $\epsilon$)}\\ &= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \epsilon \end{aligned} xt​​=αt​​xt​+1−αt​​ϵ , ϵ∼N(0,I)=αt​​(αt−1​​xt−1​+1−αt−1​​ϵ)+1−αt​​ϵ=... (reorganize the variances of the standard noise ϵ)=αˉt​​x0​+1−αˉt​​ϵ​

Reverse Process

The reverse process aims to train a model that can denoise the noisy samples xtx_txt​ back to the original data x0x_0x0​. Which means that we want to minimize the KL divergence between the true posterior q(xt−1∣xt)q(x_{t-1} | x_t)q(xt−1​∣xt​) and the model’s approximation p(xt−1∣xt)p(x_{t-1} | x_t)p(xt−1​∣xt​).
The true posterior is given by:

q(xt−1∣xt)=q(xt∣xt−1)q(xt−1∣x0)q(xt∣x0)=N(xt;αtxt−1,βtI)N(xt−1;αˉt−1x0,βˉt−1I)N(xt;αˉtx0,βˉtI)\begin{aligned} q(x_{t-1} | x_t) &= \frac{q(x_t | x_{t-1}) q(x_{t-1} | x_0)}{q(x_t | x_0)} \\ &= \frac{\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\alpha_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I) \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} x_0, \bar{\beta}_{t-1} I)}{\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} x_0, \bar{\beta}_t I)} \end{aligned} q(xt−1​∣xt​)​=q(xt​∣x0​)q(xt​∣xt−1​)q(xt−1​∣x0​)​=N(xt​;αˉt​​x0​,βˉ​t​I)N(xt​;αt​​xt−1​,βt​I)N(xt−1​;αˉt−1​​x0​,βˉ​t−1​I)​​

This is because we don’t know q(xt)q(x_t)q(xt​) and q(xt−1)q(x_{t-1})q(xt−1​) directly, but we can compute q(xt−1∣x0)q(x_{t-1} | x_0)q(xt−1​∣x0​) and q(xt∣x0)q(x_t | x_0)q(xt​∣x0​) in closed form.
Then, it’s straight forward to define a KL divergence loss:

LKL=Ex0,ϵ,t[DKL(q(xt−1∣xt)∣∣pθ(xt−1∣xt))]=Ex0,ϵ,t[DKL(N(xt−1;μt(xt,x0),Σt)∣∣N(xt−1;μθ(xt,t),Σθ(xt,t)))]\begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} &= \mathbb{E}_{x_0, \epsilon, t} \left[ D_{\text{KL}}(q(x_{t-1} | x_t) || p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)) \right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{x_0, \epsilon, t} \left[ D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_t(x_t, x_0), \Sigma_t) || \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_\theta(x_t,t))) \right] \end{aligned} LKL​​=Ex0​,ϵ,t​[DKL​(q(xt−1​∣xt​)∣∣pθ​(xt−1​∣xt​))]=Ex0​,ϵ,t​[DKL​(N(xt−1​;μt​(xt​,x0​),Σt​)∣∣N(xt−1​;μθ​(xt​,t),Σθ​(xt​,t)))]​

We know that

μt(xt,x0)=αˉt−1βt1−αˉtx0+αt(1−αˉt−1)1−αˉtxtΣt=βt1−αˉt−11−αˉtI\begin{aligned} \mu_t(x_t, x_0) &= \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_t}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t} (1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_t \\ \Sigma_t &= \beta_t\frac{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} I \end{aligned} μt​(xt​,x0​)Σt​​=1−αˉt​αˉt−1​​βt​​x0​+1−αˉt​αt​​(1−αˉt−1​)​xt​=βt​1−αˉt​1−αˉt−1​​I​

For detailed derivation of the above two terms, see Understanding Diffusion Models: A Unified Perspective, page 12.

Then it’s easy to compute the KL divergence:

DKL(N(xt−1;μt(xt,x0),Σt)∣∣N(xt−1;μθ(xt,t),Σθ(t)))=12(−1+log⁡Σt−log⁡Σθ(xt,t)+trΣtΣθ(xt,t)+(μθ(xt,t)−μt(xt,x0))TΣθ(xt,t)−1(μθ(xt,t)−μt(xt,x0)))\begin{aligned} D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; & \mu_t(x_t, x_0), \Sigma_t) || \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_\theta(t))) \\ % \frac{1}{2} ( \log \frac{\det(\Sigma_\theta(t))}{\det(\Sigma_t)} + \text{tr}(\Sigma_\theta(t)^{-1} \Sigma_t) + (\mu_\theta(x_t, t) & - \mu_t(x_t, x_0))^T \Sigma_\theta(t)^{-1} (\mu_\theta(x_t, t) - \mu_t(x_t, x_0)) - d ) \\ = & \frac{1}{2} ( -1 + \log \Sigma_t - \log \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t) + tr{\frac{\Sigma_t}{\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)}} + \\ &(\mu_\theta(x_t, t) - \mu_t(x_t, x_0))^T\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)^{-1} (\mu_\theta(x_t, t) - \mu_t(x_t, x_0)) ) \end{aligned} DKL​(N(xt−1​;=​μt​(xt​,x0​),Σt​)∣∣N(xt−1​;μθ​(xt​,t),Σθ​(t)))21​(−1+logΣt​−logΣθ​(xt​,t)+trΣθ​(xt​,t)Σt​​+(μθ​(xt​,t)−μt​(xt​,x0​))TΣθ​(xt​,t)−1(μθ​(xt​,t)−μt​(xt​,x0​)))​

Here we can see that the model is similar to a hierarchical VAE, which outputs the μθ(xt,t)\mu_\theta(x_t, t)μθ​(xt​,t) and Σθ(xt,t)\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)Σθ​(xt​,t) at each step.

However, in practice, we often assume a fixed variance Σθ(xt,t)=ΣtI\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t) = \Sigma_t IΣθ​(xt​,t)=Σt​I (Why we can do that? because the optimal KL divergence forces them to be equal) and only train the mean μθ(xt,t)\mu_\theta(x_t, t)μθ​(xt​,t), which simplifies the loss to a MSE loss between the posterior means:

LMSE=Ex0,ϵ,t[∥μθ(xt,t)−μt(xt,x0)∥2]\begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} &= \mathbb{E}_{x_0, \epsilon, t} \left[ \| \mu_\theta(x_t, t) - \mu_t(x_t, x_0) \|^2 \right] \end{aligned} LMSE​​=Ex0​,ϵ,t​[∥μθ​(xt​,t)−μt​(xt​,x0​)∥2]​

Or can be reformulated into the MSE between the denoised sample xθ(xt,t)x_{\theta}(x_t, t)xθ​(xt​,t) and the original sample x0x_0x0​ by setting:

We have: μt(xt,x0)=αˉt−1βt1−αˉtx0+αt(1−αˉt−1)1−αˉtxtWe set: μθ(xt,t)=αˉt−1βt1−αˉtxθ(xt,t)+αt(1−αˉt−1)1−αˉtxt\begin{aligned} \text{We have: } \mu_t(x_t, x_0) &= \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_t}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t} (1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_t \\ \text{We set: }\mu_\theta(x_t, t) = & \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_t}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_\theta(x_t, t) + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t} (1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_t \end{aligned} We have: μt​(xt​,x0​)We set: μθ​(xt​,t)=​=1−αˉt​αˉt−1​​βt​​x0​+1−αˉt​αt​​(1−αˉt−1​)​xt​1−αˉt​αˉt−1​​βt​​xθ​(xt​,t)+1−αˉt​αt​​(1−αˉt−1​)​xt​​

Then we can rewrite the loss as:

LMSE∝Ex0,ϵ,t[∥xθ(xt,t)−x0∥2]\begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} \propto & \mathbb{E}_{x_0, \epsilon, t} \left[ \| x_\theta(x_t, t) - x_0 \|^2 \right] \\ \end{aligned} LMSE​∝​Ex0​,ϵ,t​[∥xθ​(xt​,t)−x0​∥2]​

Implementation in PyTorch

Warning: The following code is a simplified version of the OpenAI’s official implementation, which is more complex and includes many optimizations. The code here is meant to illustrate the key concepts and equations and for better readability.

Beta Schedule:

The beta schedule is crucial for the forward diffusion process. It defines how the noise is added to the data at each step. Common schedules include linear and cosine. People prefer cosine schedules because they provide a smoother transition at the endpoints (the beginning and end of the diffusion process), when the noise is very small or very large. This helps to stabilize the training process and improve the quality of the generated samples.

Reverse Diffusion Process:

The reverse diffusion process is defined by the posterior distribution pθ(xt−1∣xt)p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)pθ​(xt−1​∣xt​). Remember that we need parameters μθ(xt,t)\mu_\theta(x_t, t)μθ​(xt​,t) and Σθ(xt,t)\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)Σθ​(xt​,t), which are computed from the denoised sample xθ(xt,t)x_\theta(x_t, t)xθ​(xt​,t) given model output xθ(xt,t)x_\theta(x_t, t)xθ​(xt​,t), ϵθ(xt,t)\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)ϵθ​(xt​,t), or sθ(xt,t)s_\theta(x_t, t)sθ​(xt​,t) (the predicted noise, denoised sample, or the score function). Therefore, given xθ(xt,t)x_\theta(x_t, t)xθ​(xt​,t), we can compute μθ(xt,t)\mu_\theta(x_t, t)μθ​(xt​,t) and Σθ(xt,t)\Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)Σθ​(xt​,t) with the previously defined q_posterior_mean_variance(self, x_start, x_t, t):
