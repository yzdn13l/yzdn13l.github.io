Hilbert space with a norm

A Hilbert space is a complete inner product space, which means it is a vector space equipped with an inner product that allows us to define lengths and angles. The inner product induces a norm, which is a function that assigns a non-negative length to each vector in the space. The norm is defined as follows:

The Hilbert space of functions

The Hilbert space of functions is a space of square-integrable functions, which means that the integral of the square of the absolute value of the function is finite. The inner product in this space is defined as:

⟨f,g⟩=∫f(x)g(x)‾dx\langle f, g \rangle = \int f(x) \overline{g(x)} dx⟨f,g⟩=∫f(x)g(x)​dx

where g(x)‾\overline{g(x)}g(x)​ is the complex conjugate of g(x)g(x)g(x). The norm is defined as:

∥f∥=⟨f,f⟩=∫∣f(x)∣2dx\|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f, f \rangle} = \sqrt{\int |f(x)|^2 dx}∥f∥=⟨f,f⟩​=∫∣f(x)∣2dx​

The Fourier basis

The Fourier basis is a set of functions that are orthogonal with respect to the inner product defined above. The Fourier basis consists of complex exponentials of the form:

ϕn(x)=einx\phi_n(x) = e^{i n x}ϕn​(x)=einx

where nnn is an integer. The inner product of two Fourier basis functions is given by:

⟨ϕn,ϕm⟩=∫einxeimx‾dx=∫ei(n−m)xdx\langle \phi_n, \phi_m \rangle = \int e^{i n x} \overline{e^{i m x}} dx = \int e^{i (n - m) x} dx⟨ϕn​,ϕm​⟩=∫einxeimxdx=∫ei(n−m)xdx

If two functions are orthogonal, their inner product is zero. The Fourier basis is orthonormal, which means that the inner product of a function with itself is equal to 1:

⟨ϕn,ϕn⟩=∫einxeinx‾dx=∫1dx=L\langle \phi_n, \phi_n \rangle = \int e^{i n x} \overline{e^{i n x}} dx = \int 1 dx = L⟨ϕn​,ϕn​⟩=∫einxeinxdx=∫1dx=L

where LLL is the length of the domain. The Fourier basis is complete, which means that any function in the Hilbert space can be expressed as a linear combination of the Fourier basis functions.
