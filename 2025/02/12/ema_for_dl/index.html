Exponential Moving Averages in Deep Learning

Posted on 2025-02-12 Edited on 2025-05-04 In DL Heuristics

Simple moving average (SMA)

In financial math, the SMA is simply the moving average of previous k data points.

where pnp_npn​ is the price at time n.

Exponential moving average (EMA)

The EMA is a weighted average of previous data points, where the weights decrease exponentially. The formula for EMA is given by:

EMAn=αpn+(1−α)EMAn−1\text{EMA}_n = \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-1}EMAn​=αpn​+(1−α)EMAn−1​

where α\alphaα is the smoothing factor, which determines the weight given to the most recent data point. The value of α\alphaα is typically between 0 and 1, and it can be calculated as:

α=2N+1\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1} α=N+12​

where N is the number of periods over which the EMA is calculated.

Why is it exponential? Reformulating the equation we can see that:

EMAn=αpn+(1−α)EMAn−1=αpn+(1−α)(αpn−1+(1−α)EMAn−2)=αpn+(1−α)αpn−1+(1−α)2EMAn−2=αpn+(1−α)αpn−1+(1−α)2αpn−2+...=∑i=0∞(1−α)iαpn−i\begin{aligned} \text{EMA}_n &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-1} \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \left( \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha) \text{EMA}_{n-2} \right) \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha)^2 \text{EMA}_{n-2} \\ &= \alpha p_n + (1-\alpha) \alpha p_{n-1} + (1-\alpha)^2 \alpha p_{n-2} + ... \\ &= \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} (1-\alpha)^i \alpha p_{n-i} \\ \end{aligned} EMAn​​=αpn​+(1−α)EMAn−1​=αpn​+(1−α)(αpn−1​+(1−α)EMAn−2​)=αpn​+(1−α)αpn−1​+(1−α)2EMAn−2​=αpn​+(1−α)αpn−1​+(1−α)2αpn−2​+...=i=0∑∞​(1−α)iαpn−i​​

Here the shared weight α\alphaα is multiplied by the exponentially decreasing factor (1−α)i(1-\alpha)^i(1−α)i. Meaning that the previous data has a weight that decreases exponentially with time.

Why is it useful in deep learning?

Weight Update with EMA

θEMA=αθ+(1−α)θEMA\theta_{EMA} = \alpha \theta + (1-\alpha) \theta_{EMA} θEMA​=αθ+(1−α)θEMA​

where θ\thetaθ is the model’s parameters, and θEMA\theta_{EMA}θEMA​ is the EMA of the model’s parameters.

First of all, the optimization of a neural network is a non-convex problem. The loss function is not smooth and has many local minima or saddle points. Thus, SGD adds noise to the optimization process, which can help escape local minima and saddle points. However, this noise can also be harmful to the model’s performance. The EMA can help smooth out the noise and provide a more stable estimate of the model’s parameters.

EMA is also particularly useful when the training process is highly stochastic by the nature of the data, the model architecture, or the training process itself. For example, in self-supervised learning, the model is trained on a large amount of unlabeled data, which can be highly variable and noisy. The EMA can help stabilize the training process and improve the model’s performance.

In Reinforcement Learning

In RL, for methods such as DDPG, the trajectories are sampled from a replay buffer collected from the main model. This coupling between the main model and the replay buffer can lead to high variance in the updates of the value function and policy. To reduce this variance, the EMA is used to maintain a separate target model that is updated with a moving average of the main model’s parameters. This target model is used to compute the target Q-values for the main model, which helps to stabilize the training process and reduce the variance in the updates.

  • OpenAI - DDPG

In GANs

In GANs, the generator and discriminator are trained in an adversarial manner. The generator tries to generate realistic samples while the discriminator tries to distinguish between real and fake samples. This can lead to instability in the training process. To stabilize the training, EMA is used for the generator’s parameters to smooth out the noise.

  • StyleGAN paper
  • BigGAN paper

In Vision Transformers (DINO) and Diffusion Transformers

In ViTs and DiTs, the training process is highly unstable due to the large number of parameters and the self-supervised training.

In DINO, the teacher weights are updated with EMA of the student weights. The author mentioned that this EMA formulates a model-ensembling similar to the Polyak-Ruppert averaging with an exponential decay, which was a standard technique in the past to improve model performance.

In DiT, the EMA is assumed to eliminate the overfitting of the model to the last seen batch, thus the learned score function is more generalized to the whole dataset. This usually improves the similarity between the generated distribution and the true distribution.
